I · Origins
[ IMAGE NEEDED ]Rizal teaching under kiosk

A dream
before
exile.

In a letter to Blumentritt dated March 31, 1890, Rizal already confided his wish to build a secular, independent school in the Philippines. After winning lottery money in September 1892, he bought 16 hectares in Talisay and built three houses: a square one where he lived with his mother, sister Trinidad, and a nephew; an octagonal one for his students; and a hexagonal one for his chickens.

II · Living Curriculum

Structure: The school functioned at both primary and secondary levels, modeled after the German gymnasium system. Enrollment grew from 14 pupils to about 24. Older boys (up to age 16) followed a high-school curriculum aimed at preparing future "cadres" for higher education; younger children had elementary instruction.

Languages: Spanish, English, French, and German. Rizal rewarded students who could name objects in the most languages. Mathematics: arithmetic.
[ IMAGE NEEDED ]
Hands-on learning: Collecting insects, gathering shells, diving for fish as lessons in botany and zoology. Rizal made his own teaching materials—blackboards, maps, writing tools—and used natural specimens as pedagogical aids. He adapted lessons to each student's aptitude, noting one nephew preferred farming while another had scholarly bent.

III · Himno a Talisay
[ IMAGE NEEDED ]Manuscript—Himno a Talisay, 1895

Written
to be
sung.

Composed in 1895 and dedicated to his pupils—poetry as sound, meant to be carried in children's voices. Rizal understood education was not merely cerebral; it passed through emotion, music, and the body. The school was alive.

IV · Teaching Practice

Informal setting: Classes held in the square house or in a kiosk built on a hillside as his private retreat. Rizal often taught from a hammock while boys sat around him on the floor, grass, or a bench.

Assessment: The top-scoring student at any time received a symbolic "place of honor." Periodic exams administered with outside examiners. Top performers won prizes: pen, book, net, or rifle.
[ IMAGE NEEDED ]
Pedagogical creativity: He invented La Sibila Cumana, a fortune-telling board game to prompt students to think about bigger life questions. He told ghost stories at night, then sent students out one by one into the dark to retrieve something hidden—building courage.

Why Progressive?

Integration with life: Rizal integrated education directly into community and working life, treating school not as separate from "real life" but as part of it. This pioneered what Philippine educator Maria Luisa Doronila would later formalize as "school-based management" and "community-based education."

Individual adaptation: Rizal adapted lessons to each nephew's aptitude, noting one preferred farming over books ("It is necessary that there be some to cultivate the land"), while another had a more scholarly bent.
[ IMAGE NEEDED ]
Lasting impact: When Rizal left for Manila, he gifted 6 of his 16 hectares in Talisay to his pupil and valet, Jose Acopiado. His star pupil, Jose Aseniero, went on to become governor of Zamboanga province (1925–1928).
